Diffraction

What is Diffraction?

Diffraction is the spreading of a wave as it passes through a gap or around an obstacle. The amount of diffraction depends on the relationship between the wavelength of the wave and the size of the gap. When the gap is much larger than the wavelength, the wave spreads out very little. However, when the gap is similar in size to or smaller than the wavelength, the wave spreads out significantly. Longer wavelengths diffract more than shorter wavelengths, which is why sound waves can often be heard around corners while light waves generally travel in straight lines. Understanding diffraction helps explain how waves behave when they encounter obstacles and openings in their path.

Notes
Increasing the wavelength (slit width constant) The wavelength becomes larger relative to the slit width. Diffraction increases, causing the transmitted wave energy to spread over a wider range of angles. The total energy passing through the slit remains approximately the same, but it is distributed over a larger area. In this simulation: The spacing between successive wavefronts increases, while the overall wavefront shape changes very little because interference is not modelled.

Decreasing the wavelength (slit width constant) The wavelength becomes smaller relative to the slit width. Diffraction decreases, so more of the transmitted wave energy remains concentrated in the forward direction. The total energy passing through the slit remains approximately the same. In this simulation: The spacing between successive wavefronts decreases, while the overall wavefront shape changes very little.

Decreasing the slit width (wavelength constant) The slit becomes smaller relative to the wavelength. Diffraction increases, causing the transmitted wave energy to spread over a wider range of angles. Because the opening is smaller, less of the incident wave energy passes through the slit. In this simulation: The outgoing wavefront becomes noticeably more curved, demonstrating Huygens' Principle.

Increasing the slit width (wavelength constant) The slit becomes larger relative to the wavelength. Diffraction decreases, so more of the transmitted wave energy continues straight ahead. Because the opening is larger, more of the incident wave energy passes through the slit. In this simulation: The outgoing wavefront becomes flatter, approaching the shape of the original plane wave.

Simulation note: This simulation only shows the geometric aspect of diffraction (where the wavefronts can propagate) and not the intensity distribution (the amount of energy that reaches each direction) as it does not take into account interference or intensity. Therefore, although changing the wavelength does not have a noticable change on the shape of the wavefront, it affects the energy distributed in each direction and therefore still affects the strength of diffraction.
